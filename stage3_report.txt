Abstract— The rapid advancement of AI-generated media has
made deepfake videos indistinguishable from authentic content,
posing serious threats to digital trust, security, and legal integrity.
Existing deepfake detection systems are often limited by framelevel analysis and lack of interpretability. This paper presents an
explainable deepfake detection framework that leverages
temporal modeling to overcome these limitations. The proposed
system integrates FFmpeg-based video normalization,
RetinaFace-based facial detection, ResNeXt-50 feature extraction,
and CNN-based temporal aggregation to classify videos as real or
fake. Additionally, an explainability module identifies specific
frames contributing to the final prediction, generating forensicstyle reports in PDF format. By combining temporal reasoning
with interpretable outputs, this work aims to improve the
reliability and transparency of deepfake detection systems.
I. INTRODUCTION
The emergence of deep learning-based generative models
has led to a new class of digital threats known as deepfakes,
where a person’s face or identity is manipulated in video content
using techniques such as Generative Adversarial Networks
(GANs). These synthetic media have been used in political
misinformation campaigns, financial fraud, and manipulation of
digital evidence, raising serious concerns about trust in online
content.
Early deepfake detection methods relied on identifying
visual artifacts such as unnatural blinking, boundary
inconsistencies, or lighting mismatches. However, recent
advances in generative models have significantly reduced these
artifacts, making detection increasingly difficult, even for
automated systems.
Despite growing research in this area, many existing
detection approaches remain limited. Many systems operate at
the frame level, treating each frame as an independent image and
ignoring temporal relationships. Additionally, most models
function as black boxes, providing only a single prediction score
without explaining the reasoning behind the decision. These
limitations reduce both the robustness and trustworthiness of
current detection systems. To address these challenges, this
project proposes a deepfake detection framework that
emphasizes temporal reasoning and explainability. By analyzing
inter-frame differences rather than isolated images, the system
captures temporal inconsistencies that are difficult for
generative models to reproduce consistently. Furthermore, the
modular design allows individual components to be updated
independently, and the explainability module provides
interpretable outputs suitable for high-stakes applications.
II. EXISTING PROBLEMS
A. Limitations of Frame-Level Detection
Most existing deepfake detection methods rely on framelevel classification, where individual video frames are analyzed
independently using spatial features. While computationally
efficient, such approaches inherently ignore temporal
dependencies between consecutive frames. As modern deepfake
generation techniques continue to improve, they can produce
highly realistic individual frames, making purely spatial
detection increasingly unreliable.
Recent studies show that frame-level detectors tend to learn
dataset-specific artifacts, such as blending boundaries or
compression noise, rather than intrinsic forgery characteristics,
resulting in poor generalization across datasets [1]. Furthermore,
these methods fail to capture temporal inconsistencies such as
unstable facial boundaries, inconsistent illumination, and
irregular motion patterns that frequently occur in manipulated
videos but are difficult for generative models to reproduce
consistently over time [1].
Importantly, the effectiveness of incorporating temporal
information has been empirically validated. Rowan and Pears
demonstrated that models leveraging temporal dependencies
achieve statistically significant improvements over framedependent approaches (p < 0.05), highlighting the importance of
spatial-temporal feature learning in deepfake detection [2]. The
lack of temporal modeling therefore limits the ability of framebased approaches to exploit motion coherence and dynamic
facial behavior, which are critical cues for distinguishing real
from manipulated content.
B. Lack of Interpretability in Predictions
Many deepfake detection systems operate as black-box
models, providing only a single prediction score indicating
whether a video is real or fake. While effective for classification,
such outputs lack transparency and do not provide insight into
the reasoning behind the model’s decision.
Ensuring explainability remains a primary challenge in
deepfake detection systems, as most models fail to provide
interpretable justifications for their predictions. This limitation
restricts the ability of users to understand which features or
regions of the input contributed to the final decision.
Without clear reasoning and visual evidence, users cannot
effectively validate model outputs, leading to reduced trust and
adoption. Consequently, improving explainability is essential
for developing reliable and trustworthy deepfake detection
frameworks.
III. SOCIAL IMPACT AND ETHICAL MOTIVATION
Deepfake technology’s misuse is already causing concrete
harm. For example, a Hong Kong finance worker was tricked by
a deepfake video call of his company’s CFO into transferring
over $25 million to fraudsters [3]. Industry reports further
highlight the growing scale of this threat, noting a rise in AIdriven identity fraud and deepfake-enabled scams across
financial and corporate sectors [3]. Such realistic forgeries can
mimic trusted individuals, making even cautious employees
vulnerable. These incidents illustrate that deepfakes pose
immediate economic risks by enabling large-scale scams and
extortion.
Meanwhile, deepfakes are amplifying misinformation and
manipulating public opinion. In a 2025 consumer survey, 68%
of respondents said they worried about deepfakes’ societal
impact, with “fake news” and misinformation cited as their top
concern [4]. Political campaigns have already deployed AIgenerated videos, and analysts warn that such ads can leave
voters confused or even deceived [5]. Experiments confirm that
people often fail to recognize these forgeries, allowing deepfake
ads to sway opinions [6].
Deepfakes also threaten the justice system. Courts have
already seen AI-generated videos submitted as evidence,
prompting concern among judges that they may not reliably
distinguish fakery [7]. Legal leaders note that if litigants fear any
evidence might be AI-altered, public trust in judicial outcomes
is jeopardized.
These threats motivate our project to build an accessible,
open-source deepfake detection system. By making the tool
public, we enable researchers, journalists, and legal
professionals to verify media authenticity. Crucially, our
detector emphasizes explainability. Explainable AI is especially
important in high-stakes contests, where automated flags must
be transparent and contestable by human experts. We also
prioritize accuracy and robustness to minimize false positives
and false negatives. In this way, our design provides
interpretability and emphasizes human oversight, so that users
can understand, trust, and fairly evaluate each result rather than
treating the system as an infallible judge.
IV. PROPOSED SOLUTION
The proposed deepfake detection system is designed as a
modular, multi-stage pipeline that analyzes videos and produces
interpretable outputs. Unlike conventional approaches that
classify individual frames independently, the system focuses on
temporal consistency across sequences of frames, enabling it to
detect subtle artifacts that emerge over time rather than within a
single image.
Figure 1: System Architecture
The first stage performs video preprocessing using the
FFmpeg framework. Uploaded videos are decoded and
normalized to a consistent frame rate, resolution, and pixel
format. Additionally, light denoising is applied to reduce
compression artifacts introduced by different platforms. This
normalization step is crucial to ensure that downstream models
receive standardized inputs, thereby improving robustness and
reducing variance caused by heterogeneous data sources.
Following preprocessing, individual frames are passed to a
face detection module based on RetinaFace, which generates
bounding boxes and facial landmarks for all detected faces.
These landmarks are then used in conjunction with OpenCV to
crop, resize, and geometrically align each face. Alignment
ensures that facial regions are consistently positioned across
frames, minimizing geometric noise such as rotation or scale
variations. This step is essential because the downstream feature
extractor is sensitive to spatial inconsistencies that are unrelated
to deepfake artifacts.
The aligned face crops are then passed into a ResNeXt-50
model [8] without the classification head, which generates high
dimensional feature embeddings for each frame. These
embeddings capture rich visual information, including facial
texture, structural geometry, and bounding coherence. By
leveraging a pretrained convolutional architecture, the system
extracts semantically meaningful representations that are more
robust than raw pixel inputs.
To model temporal relationships, the extracted features are
fed into a Temporal CNN-based module. Rather than passing
raw embeddings directly, the system computes the difference
between consecutive frame embeddings within a window of five
frames, producing four difference vectors per window. These
difference vectors explicitly represent the change in visual
features between adjacent frames, making the model more
sensitive to temporal inconsistencies that are characteristic of
manipulated content. The difference vectors are transposed and
treated as a one-dimensional signal, with the 2048 feature
dimensions acting as channels and the four time steps as the
sequence. They are then passed through a Conv1D branch with
a kernel of size 3, ReLU activation, Batch Normalization, and
Global Average Pooling. The resulting representation is passed
through a two-layer fully connected classifier with dropout to
produce a window-level manipulation probability. This design
is computationally lightweight while remaining temporal in
reasoning, since it processes only changes between frames rather
than the absolute frames.
The system then aggregates probability scores across all
windows to produce a final video-level classification using a
simple mean. In addition to generating a binary real/fake
decision, the model tracks which frame windows contribute
most significantly to the prediction. These contributions are
compiled in an explainability module, which generates a
structured report highlighting suspicious timestamps.
The final output of the system includes a classification label
(real or fake), a confidence score, and a set of flagged
timestamps indicating regions of interest. The output can be
exported in PDF format, ensuring accessibility for both technical
users and non-technical stakeholders.
V. TESTING, EVALUATION, AND BENCHMARKING
To rigorously evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed
deepfake detection system, we assess its performance using both
quantitative and comparative benchmarks.
A. Dataset and Experiment Setup
The system is trained and evaluated using the
FaceForensics++ (C23 compression) dataset, accessed via
Kaggle. The dataset contains 1,000 original videos and 1,000
Deepfake videos generated using the autoencoder based faceswap technique. All videos were preprocessed through the same
pipeline described in the Section IV: FFmpeg decoding at 10
FPS with light denoising, RetinaFace face detection, OpenCVbased cropping and geometric alignment, and ResNeXt-50
feature extraction (final classification head was removed,
producing 2048-dimensional embeddings per frame). These perframe embeddings were saved as NumPy arrays and treated as
our new dataset to train and test the models.
Figure 2: Dataset preparation for training
The resulting feature vectors were split at the video level into
80% training, 10% validation, and 10% test sets. Critically, the
same split was used for training and testing both our temporal
based CNN model as well as baseline model.
B. Evaluation metrics
While overall accuracy provides a basic measure of
performance, it is insufficient for high-stakes applications such
as fraud detection and legal verification. Therefore, we
evaluated the models using multiple metrics as listed below.
• Accuracy: Measures the overall proportion of correctly
classified videos.
• AUC-ROC (Area Under the Receiver Operating
Characteristic Curve): Measures models’ ability to
separate real and fake videos.
• Precision: Evaluates how many videos classified as
deepfakes are actually fake, which is critical for
minimizing false accusations.
• Recall: Measures how many actual fake videos are
correctly identified, which is critical for minimizing
missed detections.
• F1-score: F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision and
recall, providing a balanced single metric.
• AUC-PR (Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve): A
high AUC-PR indicates that model can identify most
deepfakes (Recall) while maintaining a low rate of false
alarms (Precision).
• False Positive Rate (FPR): Indicates how often real
videos are incorrectly flagged as fake.
• False Negative Rate (FNR): Indicates how often
deepfakes are missed by the system.
To handle false predictions, the system outputs confidence
scores for each classification. Predictions with low confidence
or those near the decision boundary are flagged for manual
review. This reduces the risk of critical misclassifications,
particularly in high-stakes scenarios such as legal verification.
C. Baseline Comparison
The proposed temporal CNN was compared against a framelevel MLP baseline designed for direct and fair comparison. For
fairness in comparison, instead of external third-party model,
this baseline was trained from scratch on the same dataset, using
the same feature extraction pipeline and the same
train/validation/test split. This approach was chosen to eliminate
factors such as difference in preprocessing, data distribution, or
training data. The MLP baseline (SimpleFrameMLP) takes
individual 2048-dimensional frame embeddings as input and
passes them through two fully-connected layers (2048->256->1)
with ReLU activation, followed by a sigmoid output. In this
way, each frame was classified independently, and the videolevel prediction was obtained by mean pooling the frame-level
probabilities. For both the temporal based CNN model and
baseline model, videos with deepfake probabilities of values
greater than or equal to 0.50 were considered as deepfake and
less than 0.50 were considered real to compare the metrics.

D. Limitations, Generalization, and Future Work
The proposed system is trained and evaluated on a subset of
the FaceForensics++ dataset consisting of 1000 original and
1000 Deepfake videos, where the Deepfake category represents
a single manipulation method: autoencoder-based faceswapping. While computationally efficient for training and
testing, it introduces two limitations.
First, the dataset scope is narrow. FaceForensics++ contains
multiple manipulation methods such as Face2Face, FaceSwap,
and NaturalTextures. Our system was trained exclusively on
one face swap method due to limitations in compute resources
and may have learned features specific to that generation
technique rather than general indicators of temporal
inconsistency, limiting its ability to generalize to unseen
manipulation types. Evaluation on a more diverse dataset and
cross-dataset evaluation is necessary to test true generalization
which we have planned for future works.
Second, we found that the quality of the deepfakes in this
particular subset was mixed. Some inspection revealed that
many of the manipulated videos contain visible artifacts such as
unnatural boundary blending that are detectable easily even by
human eyes. This raises an important question about the
interpretation of both models’ results. If significant portion of
the fake videos were easily distinguishable from original videos
on the basis of individual frames alone, a frame-level model may
achieve competitive performance not by generalizing features
but by exploiting frame based artifacts specific to the dataset.
This could also partly explain why the baseline performs
comparable to the temporal based CNN on most metrics.
Conversely, this observation may not hold true under rigorous
analysis i.e. the frame-based model could have actual
discriminative power. This ambiguity further motivates our
future works including evaluation on higher-quality and more
recent deepfakes generated with diverse manipulation
techniques.
Deepfake generation techniques continue to evolve,
particularly with the rise of adversarial methods designed to
bypass standard classifiers, making robust detection a
significant challenge. By employing temporal modeling and
feature-level analysis, the system aims to increase the difficulty
for generators to maintain consistency over frames and reduce
reliance on pixel-level artifacts that can be easily manipulated.
The results from our experiments showed that temporal
modeling offers meaningful improvements over frame-based
technique and the gap may widen on higher-quality deepfake
datasets.
Future work for this system includes evaluation on
additional FaceForensics++ manipulation types and external
datasets to improve and test cross-dataset generalization. Also,
future works could include experimenting with longer temporal
windows, overlapping windows, and even threshold calibration
to improve precision-recall tradeoff without retraining.
VI. ALTERNATIVE APPROACHES AND EXTENSIONS
The proposed system uses a Temporal based CNN operating
on inter-frame difference vectors, but it is not the only available
approach for this task. One potential alternative is LSTM
networks. Similarly, alternative approaches could include
experimenting with different window sizes as well. Similarly,
Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) is another alternative, which
simplifies the LSTM architecture by reducing the number of
gating mechanisms while achieving comparable performance.
For example, R. Hande et al. directly compared CNN+LSTM
and CNN+GRU pipeline to analyze sequential data and found
that both architectures achieved competitive results [11].
Further, we could look into diverse models and create an
ensemble of different models to evaluate whether their
combined outputs yield better accuracy in detecting instability
over single models.
VII. SYSTEM ANALYSIS AND DECOMPOSITION
The deepfake detection system is decomposed into nine
distinct modules, each responsible for a well-defined stage of the
detection pipeline. This modular architecture promotes
separation of concerns, enabling individual components to be
developed, tested, and updated independently.
A. WebInterface
The WebInterface is the front-facing module that allows
users to interact with the system even without any programming
knowledge. It provides a video upload form, displays the final
verdict and confidence score, and allows users to jump to a
specific timestamp in the uploaded video and analyze the
probability of that part of the video being deepfake as output by
the system. Users can also download the generated report in PDF
format from this module.
B. VideoInputHandler
The VideoInputHandler serves as the entry point to the
system, responsible for receiving user-submitted video files
through the web interface. It validates the uploaded file format
and size, and then passes the raw video to the preprocessing
stage. This module abstracts the file I/O for the rest of the
pipeline, ensuring that the downstream modules always receive
a video in a valid format.
C. FFmpegPreprocessor
The FFmpegPreprocessor normalizes the incoming video to
a consistent format before any analysis takes place. It decodes
the video into individual frames, resamples to a target frame rate,
resizes to a standard resolution, and applies light denoising to
reduce compression artifacts introduced by different platforms.
This normalization is critical because the downstream models
are sensitive to resolution and quality inconsistencies that are
unrelated to deepfake artifacts. FFmpeg is used as the
underlying processing engine due to its broad codec support and
efficiency.
D. FaceDetection
The FaceDetection module applies the RetinaFace model to
each preprocessed frame to locate all human faces present. It
produces bounding boxes defining the region of each detected
face and extracts five-point facial landmarks (eyes, nose, and
mouth corners) per detection. These landmarks are passed to the
next module.
E. FaceCropAndAlignment
The FaceCropAndAlignment module takes the bounding
boxes and landmarks from the FaceDetection module and
produces a geometrically standardized face crop for each
detection. It uses OpenCV to crop the face region from the full
frame and apply transformation using the landmarks
to normalize rotation, scale, and translation. Consistent
alignment across frames is essential because the ResNeXt-50
model in feature extractor is sensitive to geometric variations
unrelated to manipulation artifacts.
F. FeatureExtractor
The FeatureExtractor module passes each aligned face crop
through a pretrained ResNeXt-50 convolutional neural network
to produce a high-dimensional feature embedding. The
ResNeXt-50 architecture, pretrained on ImageNet, captures rich
spatial representations including facial texture, structural
geometry, and boundary coherence. The final layer of the model
is adapted to output a fixed-length embedding vector that
encodes the visual characteristics of each individual face crop.
These vectors are passed to the temporal modeling stage.
G. TemporalAnalyzer
The TemporalAnalyzer is the core detection module. Rather
than classifying individual frames, it analyzes sequences of
feature vectors using a Temporal CNN trained on the
FaceForensics++ dataset. A non-overlapping sliding window
groups consecutive frame embeddings into window of 5 frames.
For each window, the difference vectors between the
consecutive embeddings are computed, producing 4 difference
vectors of dimension 2048. These are transposed to shape (2048,
4) and passed through the model to produce manipulation
probability for each window, which is later combined to produce
a video-level verdict using a simple mean.
H. ExplainabilityModule
The ExplainabilityModule transforms the raw probability
scores produced by the TemporalAnalyzer into interpretable,
human-readable evidence. It maps each window’s decision back
to the corresponding frame indices in the original video, flags
temporal segments that exceed a suspicion threshold, and
produces a probability distribution over time. This allows users
to identify exactly which portions of the video drove the model’s
decision, rather than only a binary verdict.
I. ReportGenerator
The ReportGenerator compiles the verdict, confidence score,
flagged timestamps, and probability distribution graph produced
by the ExplainabilityModule into a structured, shareable report.
Reports are generated in PDF format.

2) Class Relationships: The VideoInputHandler and
FFmpegPreprocessor share a 1-to-1 association because each
video upload triggers exactly one preprocessing task, and there
is no scenario where a single upload triggers multiple
preprocessors. This keeps the pipeline straightforward and
simple per video upload.
The FFmpegPreprocessor and FaceDetection share a 1-to1..*, meaning one FFmpeg preprocessing task produces one or
more frames that are passed to FaceDetection class. The
multiplicity on the FaceDetection side is 1..* rather than exactly
1 because a video always produces at least one frame, and
usually many. This reflects the reality that the preprocessor
outputs a frame array, not a single object.
FaceDetection and FaceCropAndAlignment share a 1-to-1
association. While FaceDetection may return multiple bounding
boxes per frame, the crop and alignment operation is applied
uniformly to each detection in sequence. The two classes are
closely related in the design, and every detection must be aligned
before feature extraction, so they have a 1-to-1 relationship.
FaceCropAndAlignment and FeatureExtractor also share a
1-to-1 association because each aligned face crop is passed
directly to the feature extractor, which processes it and returns
exactly one embedding vector.
FeatureExtractor and TemporalAnalyzer share a 1..* to 1
association. This is because temporal analyzer receives the full
sequence of embedding vectors produced across all frames
before it begins processing, and a single TemporalAnalyzer
instance must receive embeddings from multiple frames to form
even one sliding window.
TemporalAnalyzer and ExplainabilityModule share a 1-to-1
association because the TemporalAnalyzer produces one set of
window-level probability scores per video, which is then passed
to the explainability module.
ExplainabilityModule and ReportGenerator share a 1-to-1
association because the structured evidence produced by the
explainability module including the score map, flagged frame
ranges, and probability distribution is passed to ReportGenerator
in a single operation.
ReportGenerator and WebInterface share a 1-to-1
association since exactly one compiled report is delivered to the
web interface for display and download at a time. Finally,
WebInterface closes the loop back to VideoInputHandler with a
1-to-1 association, representing the user's ability to submit a new
video from the same interface.

IX. ALGORITHMS
The deepfake detection system relies on two core algorithms
that work together within the pipeline as described below in the
flowchart diagrams.
A. Temporal Modeling
Figure 8: Temporal Modeling Algorithm
The Temporal Modeling Algorithm operates at the
TemporalAnalyzer module in our system and is responsible for
taking the embedding vectors produced by the FeatureExtractor
as input and producing a mapping from window of frames to
their respective probability of being deepfake as detected by our
model as output. The algorithm starts with the list of embedding
vectors and creates a window of 5 frames at a time if there are
sufficient frames. Then, it creates a window of 4 difference
vectors and passes it into our Temporal CNN trained on the
FaceForensics++ dataset, which analyzes the temporal
differences between frames and outputs a score that represents
the probability that the frames have been manipulated. Then, the
algorithm stores the score for the window and moves to the next
window. This process is repeated as long as there are sufficient
embedding vectors to create windows and the algorithm finally
outputs a mapping from windows of frames to their respective
probabilities of being deepfake.
B. Explainability / Frame Flagging
Figure 9: Explainability / Frame Flagging Algorithm
The Frame Flagging Algorithm compares the manipulation
scores for the windows against a threshold value to mark a
window as suspicious or not. This algorithm also maps the
scores back to its corresponding frame indices in the original
video.
X. SYSTEM INTERFACE
The system provides a web-based interface that allows users
to upload a video, view the detection results, and download a
structured report, all even without any programming knowledge.
The interface is divided into four main views.
A. Upload Screen
Figure 10: Upload Screen
The upload screen is the entry point of the application. It
presents the user with a “Choose Video File” button that opens
the computer’s file explorer, allowing selection of a video file.
Once the file is selected, the user submits it by clicking the
“Analyze Video” button, which sends the video to the system’s
backend for processing video through the detection pipeline.
B. Results Screen
Figure 11: Results UI View
After processing is complete, the results screen displays a
summary of the detection outcome. The verdict label indicates
whether the video is classified as real or likely manipulated,
accompanied by an overall probability score. Below the verdict,
the screen reports the most suspicious frame window i.e. the
temporal segment with the highest manipulation probability,
along with the Peak Score. Additional fields include Temporal
Instability, which indicates whether the model detected temporal
inconsistencies across windows, and Frames Flagged, which
reports how many frame windows exceeded the suspicion
threshold out of the total processed.
C. Video Player and Bar Graph
Figure 12: Video Player and Interactive Chart
This view displays the uploaded video alongside an
interactive bar chart. Each bar in the chart represents one frame
window, and the y-axis shows the manipulation probability
score for that window. Clicking on any bar in the chart
automatically seeks the video player to the corresponding
timestamp, allowing the user to directly inspect the portion of
the video the model flagged as suspicious. This interaction ties
the output of the model directly to the visual content, aiming to
make it easier to evaluate the model’s reasoning.
D. PDF Report
Figure 13: PDF Report
When the “Download PDF Report” button is clicked, the
system generates a formatted PDF document containing all the
information shown on the results screen. The report is designed
to be shareable with stakeholders such as legal professionals or
journalists who may need a documented record of the analysis.
XI. IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
A. Implementation Overview
The deepfake detection system was implemented as a fullstack application comprising a React-based frontend, a FastAPI
backend, and a PyTorch inference pipeline. The frontend
provides a web interface for video upload, result visualization
and report download as described in Section X, while the
backend exposes REST API endpoints that handle the detection
pipeline.
All model training was conducted on Google Colab using
GPU-accelerated environments, with feature vectors and model
checkpoints stored persistently on Google Drive. During
development and demonstration, the backend was hosted on
Google Colab itself and endpoint was exposed via Ngrok
tunneling, with frontend deployed on a web hosting platform
called railway. While not a production-grade deployment
strategy, this arrangement provided us sufficient infrastructure
for end-to-end testing and demonstration.
B. Key Implementation Techniques
For dataset preparation, 2000 videos from FaceForensics++
dataset was preprocessed across five batched Kaggle sessions to
stay within Kaggle’s GPU session time limits. Each video was
processed through the preprocessing pipeline till the feature
extraction module to get our new dataset as described in Section
V. The final output was 2048-dimensional embeddings per
frame stored as NumPy arrays. This converted the problem from
raw video processing into sequence modeling on fixed
dimensional feature vectors, allowing us to train and test models
independently of the computationally expensive video
preprocessing stage. We used the same dataset with exact same
split for training and testing both temporal based model as well
as the baseline model for fairness. For the temporal CNN, the
feature vectors were transposed to (2048, 4) shape taking only
the difference vectors and processed through a Conv1D branch
with kernel size 3, Batch Normalization, ReLU activation, and
Global Average Pooling, followed by a two-layer fully
connected classifier with dropout.
C. Completed and Remaining Functionalities
The core system functionality has been fully implemented,
including the end-to-end video processing pipeline, the
explainability module, the interactive web interface with
synchronized video player and probability bar chart, and
forensic PDF report generation.
The system was validated through informal testing with
users outside the development team, including people unfamiliar
with the underlying machine learning pipeline. Testers were
asked to upload videos through the web interface and evaluate
whether the system’s outputs were interpretable without any
technical guidance. Feedbacks confirmed that the interface was
accessible to non-technical users and that the forensic PDF
report provided sufficient context for understanding the
detection reasoning.
Remaining work is primarily focused on generalization and
robustness of the system. This includes cross-dataset evaluation
on additional FaceForensics++ manipulation types and other
external datasets, exploration of other temporal architectures
such as LSTM and GRU, experimentation with decision
threshold (currently 0.50) to improve precision-recall tradeoff,
and real-time streaming inference for live video analysis.
D. Implementation and Technical Challenges
We encountered several non-trivial challenges during
development. The most significant constraint was
computational infrastructure. Processing 2000 videos through
the full preprocessing pipeline in Kaggle approached the
allowed GPU session limits, requiring us to split the work across
five sessions. During deployment, running RetinaFace and
ResNeXt-50 simultaneously exceeded the RAM capacity on our
personal laptops that had CPUs only. Even with GPU
acceleration on personal laptops, the end-to-end processing of
20 seconds video took approximately two to three minutes
which seemed too slow for a seamless interactive experience.
These constraints motivated us to use Google Colab as the
hosting environment for testing and demonstration by using
tunneling with Ngrok.
Model development also presented its own challenges. Early
iterations of the temporal CNN with larger parameters achieved
over 99% training accuracy but dropped to 65-70% on validation
and testing accuracy, indicating that the model was memorizing
rather than generalizing. This was a direct consequence of the
relatively small dataset size of only 2000 videos, which is quite
below the scale typically used in deepfake detection research.
We addressed such challenges by reducing the model capacity.
Also, window size was fixed at five frames with no overlaps
after experiments showed that larger windows and overlaps
increased redundancy without improving performance.
The original plan for the baseline model was to use a publicly
available pretrained deepfake detector from Hugging Face [10].
However, this model and its variants produced near-random
predictions on the test dataset. This motivated us to train the
MLP based baseline model from scratch on same data and splits.
We believe that this choice ultimately produced a more
meaningful and rigorous comparison, since all variables other
than the temporal modeling were kept constant for fairness.
XII. TECHNOLOGIES
The system is built using a combination of frontend,
backend, and machine learning libraries, each chosen to handle
a specific role in the pipeline. The frontend is implemented in
React, using Axios for HTTP communication with the backend
and Chart.js for the interactive bar chart visualization. The
backend is built on FastAPI, a Python web framework. It
handles video uploads, manages the detection pipeline, and
returns forensic results to the frontend. Core pipeline
dependencies include FFmpeg for video decoding, frame
normalization, and denoising; RetinaFace for face detection and
landmark extraction; OpenCV for geometric face alignment;
PyTorch for running the ResNeXt-50 feature extractor and the
temporal CNN; and NumPy for array operations throughout the
pipeline. PDF report generation is handled by a Python library
named ReportLab.
XIII.LOCAL AND GLOBAL IMPACTS
A. Impact on Individuals
Deepfake technology poses direct and personal threats to
individuals. Non-consensual synthetic media where a person’s
face is placed into manipulated videos without their knowledge
have been used for harassment, reputational damage, and
intimated image abuse. Victims often have limited legal
recourse and face significant psychological harm. Detection
systems like the one developed in this project provide a
mechanism for individuals to verify whether video content
including them or others has been manipulated, offering a
practical means of self-protection and evidence gathering.
For everyday users, the proposed system’s emphasis on
explainability is particularly significant. Rather than returning a
black-box verdict, the system identifies which parts of the video
drove the prediction and presents this evidence in a structured,
downloadable PDF report. The system is also designed to be
accessible without programming knowledge through a webbased interface. This design helps both technical and nontechnical users to access the reasoning behind the decision rather
than being asked to simply trust an automated output. In contexts
such as personal legal disputes, employment screening, or online
harassment cases, this transparency can make a significant
difference.
B. Impact on Organizations
At the organizational level, deepfakes represent a growing
area for financial fraud and reputational attack. The widely
reported case of a Hong Kong finance worker who was deceived
by a deepfake video call into transferring over $25 million
illustrates the scale of risk that even large organizations now face
[3]. Detection systems similar to the one we propose in this
project, which are modular, deployable, and capable of
processing uploaded videos programmatically, can be integrated
into enterprise content verification workflows, media
authentication pipelines, and compliance systems.
For news organizations and media companies, the
explainability module provides a particularly valuable
capability. Journalists investigating potentially manipulated
video evidence often require not only a verdict but a documented
report. The timestamped forensic PDF report generated by the
proposed system is designed especially for this use case. Legal
and compliance teams similarly benefit from structured,
shareable evidence rather than simple Yes/No verdict.
The open-source orientation of the project further amplifies
organizational impact. By making the system publicly available,
the project enables smaller organizations, including newsrooms,
nonprofits, and civil society groups that lack resources to
develop proprietary detection systems, to access the verification
capability.
C. Impact on Society
In current society, the widespread use of deepfakes already
threatens democratic discourse, the justice system, and public
trust in media.
In democratic contexts, manipulated videos have already
been deployed in political campaigns, with analysts warning that
such content can leave voters confused or deceived [5].
Detection systems of the kind that we propose can be used by
election monitoring bodies, fact-checkers, and platform
moderators as a countermeasure against coordinated
disinformation campaigns, helping preserve the informational
integrity on which democratic participation depends. In the
justice system, the use of deepfake videos poses a direct threat
to the legal proceedings. A detection system with explainable
outputs can be used in such contexts, where human experts must
be able to interrogate and understand the detected results.
Overall, the capacity of a society to distinguish real content from
manipulated ones is one of the serious risks today and in future.
Projects including the one we propose here, that advance
detection capability and emphasize on interpretability, can
contribute to rebuilding and maintaining public trust in media.
D. Ethical Considerations
While the deepfake detection system benefits society, the
development and deployment of the system also has some
ethical considerations.
First, false positives carry real costs. In legal or journalistic
contexts, a false accusation of manipulation could harm innocent
individuals. The system addresses this through confidence score
and flagging low-confidence predictions for manual review, so
human oversight may still be needed. Second, the current system
is trained exclusively on the autoencoder-based Deepfake subset
of FaceForensics++ dataset, and responsible deployment
requires ongoing evaluation across diverse datasets to ensure
that the system does not perform disproportionately. Third, as
the deepfake generators evolve and become more sophisticated,
the detection systems should also evolve and no detection
system should be treated as a permanent solution. The modular
architecture of the proposed system where the preprocessing
components, feature extractor, and temporal layer can be
updated independently is designed with this in mind to enable
the system to evolve alongside the manipulation techniques.
Finally, the decision to make the system open-source carries
dual use implications. While public availability maximizes
benefit to legitimate users, it also makes the system’s detection
logic exposed to the adversaries seeking to defeat it.
XIV.CONCLUSION
Deepfake videos represent a growing threat to digital trust,
public discourse, and legal integrity. This project aims to address
two key limitations of existing detection systems: reliance on
frame-level analysis and lack of interpretability. The result is a
system that not only classifies videos as real or manipulated, but
also identifies which parts of the video drove that decision,
making it practical for high-stakes use cases such as legal
verification and investigative journalism.
Evaluated against a frame-level MLP baseline model trained
on identical data and splits, the proposed temporal based CNN
achieved 90.00% accuracy and a recall of 0.93, compared to
88.00% accuracy and recall of 0.86 for the baseline. The
reduction in false negative from 14% for the baseline to 7% for
the CNN model also signifies the importance of the proposed
system since missed deepfakes carry greater real world
consequences.
Overall, this work demonstrates that explicitly modeling
temporal inconsistencies between frames provides meaningful
detection improvements over frame-level analysis, and that
interpretable outputs are essential for building systems that users
can trust.
